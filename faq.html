Frequently Asked Questions

What is a form versus a schedule?

A form is a document provided by a tax authority, such as the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) in the United States, that taxpayers use to report their income, deductions, credits, and other pertinent tax information. Tax forms typically cover a broad range of tax-related topics, such as income from wages, interest, dividends, self-employment earnings, and capital gains. A schedule is an additional document that you may be required to complete if you are reporting certain types of income. Schedules are often used to report various types of income, deductions, credits, or other tax-related items in more detail than what can be accommodated directly on the main tax form.

What are the differences between filing status:

  • Single: A single filer is a taxpayer who are unmarried, divorced, or legally separated from their spouse.
  • Married joint: If the taxpaper is married, they can file taxes jointly with their spouse. If one of the spouses die during the tax year, the widow can still file the tax in this status. This filing status usually results in lower taxes compared to filing separately, and it may make you eligible for certain tax credits and deductions that you wouldn't qualify for if filing separately. Both spouses are jointly responsible for the tax liability and any errors on the return.
  • Married separate: Married couples can file separately. This filing status usually results in higher taxes compared to filing jointly and receive fewer tax benefits. However, it can benefit you in rare situations. If you or your spouse have significant out-of-pocket medical expenses, filing separately can help maximize deductions, especially if your combined income makes it difficult to exceed the AGI threshold for medical expense deductions. If your student loan repayment plan is based on your tax return income, filing separately might result in lower monthly payments, making repayment more manageable. When anticipating divorce, filing separately can prevent one spouse from being held accountable for the other's tax liabilities, particularly in cases where the divorce is not amicable.
  • Head of household: To qualify as head of household, you must be unmarried, pay more than half the cost of maintaining a home for yourself and a qualifying person (such as a dependent child or relative), and meet other criteria.
  • Qualifying surviving spouse: This filing status is available for widows and widowers for two years following the year of their spouse's death, provided they have a dependent child and meet certain conditions.

What is a digital asset?

It is an item that is created and stored digitally, such as cryptocurrencies, non-fungible tokens (NFTs), and stablecoins. Digital assets are often used to represent ownership, value, and is discoverable. If you have received or exchanged a digital asset for a fiat currency that does not belong to the United States or a foreign country, you must report this on your taxes.

What is a dependent?

Dependents are either a qualifying child or relative of the taxpayer.

  • Qualifying children must be under 19 or a full-time student under the age of 24. If they are older than 24, they must be qualified as a full-time student by the college they are attending.
  • Permanently and totally disabled children, or those who cannot work due to their illness, are dependents regardless of age.

Some examples include: a child, stepchild, brother, sister, or parent.

What are the rules for child tax credits?

To receive the child tax credit, the taxpayer must claim at least one child on their tax return.

The rules to receive this credit include:

  • The child’s Social Security number must be placed on the tax return
  • The child must be younger than 17.
  • The child must have not provided more than half of their own support for the year.
  • The child must be claimed as a dependent.
  • You cannot earn more than $200,000 a year when filing alone or $400.000 for married couples filing jointly.
The child tax credit benefits eligible parents | Internal Revenue Service

What is a W2?

The W2 is a form an employer must complete for services performed by an employee that explains income and withholdings for the year.

IRS Form W-2

How do I file taxes if I don’t have a W2?

If you have not received your W2 by February 16th, contact the IRS with the following information:

  • The employer’s name and complete address, including zip code, the employer’s identification number (if known), and telephone number
  • Your name address, including zip code, Social Security number, and telephone number
  • An estimate of the wages you earned, the federal income tax withheld, and the dates you began and ended employment.

If you still have not received it, you must use Form 4852.

If you receive a W2 after the return is filed, an amended return must be filed using Form 1040X.

Medicaid waiver payments are payments made by a certified Medicaid provider made to a home care provider for services provided to someone living in the care provider’s home.

They can also be considered as earned income for the purposes of claiming a credit or other tax benefit.

What is form 2441 (line 26)?

Form 2441 allows taxpayers who paid someone to care for their children or other qualifying person so they or their spouse could look for work during the year.

This form must be filed if the taxpayer or their spouse has received any dependent care benefits for the filing year.

What is form 8839 (line 29)?

Form 8839 is used to figure your adoption credit and any employer-provided adoption benefits that can be excluded from your income. Both the exclusion and the credit can be claimed for expenses of adopting an eligible child.

The child is eligible if:

  • They are under the age of 18.
  • They are a physically or mentally disabled person who cannot take care of themselves.

Qualified expenses include:

  • Adoption fees
  • Attorney fees
  • Court costs
  • Travel expenses (including meals and lodging) while away from home
  • Re-adoption expenses relating to the adoption of a foreign child

Qualified adoption expenses do not include expenses:

  • Funds received under any state, local, or federal program
  • That violate state or federal law
  • For carrying out a surrogate parenting arrangement
  • For the adoption of your spouse’s child
  • Reimbursed by your employer or otherwise
  • Allowed as a credit or deduction under any provision of federal income tax law

More information about this form can be found at Instructions for Form 8839 (2023) | Internal Revenue Service.

What is form 8919 (line 6)?

Form 8919 is used to figure and report the taxpayer’s share of uncollected social security and Medicare taxes due on your compensation if the taxpayer was an employee but were treated as an independent contractor by the employer.

Do I have interest and how do I know if it is taxable or tax-exempt?

Examples of taxable interest include:

  • Interest on bank accounts, money market accounts, certificates of deposit, corporate bonds, and deposited insurance dividends
  • Interest income from Treasury bills, notes and bonds. This income is subject to the federal income tax, but is exempt from all state and local income taxes
  • Savings bond interest
  • Other interest paid to you by a business, including interest received with damages or delayed death benefits

Tax-exempt interest income is not subject to income tax. This is income earned from bonds issued by states, cities, or countries and the District of Columbia.

More information can be found at Topic no. 403, Interest received | Internal Revenue Service.

Do I have dividends and how do I know if they’re qualified or ordinary?

Dividends on deposits or share accounts in cooperative banks, credit unions, domestic building and loan associations, domestic federal savings and loan associations, and mutual savings banks are qualified as taxable interest.

Ordinary dividends are paid out of earnings and profits and are treated as ordinary income.

What is an IRA distribution?

IRA distributions are income earned by an IRA that can be taken at any time. If you do take them, however, you will be subject to 10% additional tax unless you are older than age 59 ½ or meet certain exceptions.

If you intend to take your IRA distribution, you must file a Form 1040 and report the amount of money you withdrew.

How do I know if I have had a capital gain or loss?

You have had a capital gain if you sell the asset for more than your adjusted basis. You have had a capital loss if you sell the asset for less than your adjusted basis.

A capital gains rate of 0% applies if your taxable income is less than or equal to:

  • $44,625 for single and married filing separately
  • $89,250 for married filing jointly and qualifying surviving spouse
  • $59,750 for head of household

A capital gains rate of 15% applies if your taxable income is:

  • more than $44,625 but less than or equal to $492,300 for single
  • more than $44,625 but less than or equal to $276,900 for married filing separately
  • more than $89,250 but less than or equal to $553,850 for married filing jointly and qualifying surviving spouse
  • more than $59,750 but less than or equal to $523,050 for head of household

However, a capital gains rate of 20% applies to the extent that your taxable income exceeds the thresholds set for the 15% capital gain rate.

There are a few other exceptions where capital gains may be taxed at rates greater than 20%:

  • The taxable part of a gain from selling section 1202 qualified small business stock is taxed at a maximum 28% rate.
  • Net capital gains from selling collectibles (such as coins or art) are taxed at a maximum 28% rate.
  • The portion of any unrecaptured section 1250 gain from selling section 1250 real property is taxed at a maximum 25% rate.

You can report most sales and other capital transactions and calculate capital gain or loss on Form 8949 and summarize capital gains and deductible capital losses on Schedule D of Form 1040.

What is Schedule D?

Schedule D is a part of Form 1040 that is used to report the following:

  • The sale or exchange of capital assets not reported on another form or schedule
  • Gains from involuntary conversions (other than from casualty or theft) of capital assets held for business or profit
  • Capital gain distributions not reported directly on Form 1040 (or effectively connected capital gain distributions not reported directed on Form 1040-NR)
  • Non-business bad debts

What is Schedule A?

Schedule A is used in conjunction with Form 1040 to report itemized deductions.

What is earned income credit?

Earned income tax credit (EITC) helps low- to moderate-income workers and families get a tax break.

To qualify for the EITC, you must:

  • Have worked and earned income under $63,398
  • Have investment income below $11,000 in the tax year
  • Have a valid Social Security number by the due date of your tax return
  • Be a US citizen or resident alien all year
  • Not file Form 2555 for Foreign earned income
  • Meet certain rules if you are separated from your spouse and not filing a joint tax return

If you are a member of the military, there are two additional rules if you:

  • Get nontaxable military pay such as combat pay or a housing allowance or subsistence allowance
  • Were stationed outside the United States

If you are a clergy member or minister you must:

  • Include the rental value of the home you live in, or the housing allowance, if it was provided to you by the church
  • Include income you get for working as a minister if you are an employee

If you get disability payments, your payments may be qualified as earned income when you claim the EITC.

What is Form 8863, line 8?

Form 8863, line 8, allows you to calculate the American Opportunity credit which can reduce your tax liability by up to a $2,500 per student for educational expenses you or a dependent student incur during the first four years of college schooling.
